MiniBatteryLogger Help

Battery Data Sharing

Sharing is one of the activities that make the web wonderful. By sharing their battery data, MiniBatteryLogger users will unleash many powerful features of the application.

Shared Battery Data Archive

MiniBatteryLogger communicates with a web database of battery data, the Shared Battery Data Archive. It is a huge repository of measures ordered by computer model, ready to be elaborated and browsed by tecnhicians, students or just the most curious.

This data is used to elaborate statistics on average battery life, and to perform the comparison of own battery against the others.

To avoid leeching, you will be asked a pair of credentials to browse the Archive. Users who registered a license can use their username and serial key as password.

To send own data to the archive, you can press the Share your data button on the bottom side of the Comparison view, or use the menu item under the Battery menu.

To retrieve shared data from the archive, press the Get shared data button on the bottom side of the Comparison view, or use the menu item under the Battery menu.

Remote Battery Monitoring

Starting from version 1.7, MiniBatteryLogger can monitor remote batteries. When you run MiniBatteryLogger, the application will automatically discover all the other instances running over the network which share their data, and will append an entry to the left sidebar.

MiniBatteryLogger can currently monitor batteries shared by other instances of MiniBatteryLogger, MiniBatteryStatus Widget or battd daemon running on the same local network. Remote battery monitoring uses the TCP port 4153. You may need to configure your firewalls to let the traffic pass.

In the future, MiniBatteryLogger will be also able to connect to remote services over the Internet given their hostname or IP address.

To enable and configure battery data sharing, refer to the Sharing Preferences.

Remote batteries are automatically discovered and appended to the battery list on the left sidebar. You can manually disconnect them individually or globally using the action menu on the bottom of the sidebar.

To allow inbound network traffic on port 4153, refer to the “firewall” topic of the Mac Help Book..

The battd daemon

System administrators will love the standalone battd daemon. The battd daemon acts as a battery data server, making a computer remotely monitorable by MiniBatteryLogger. It will listen on TCP port 4153, quietly running on the background.

If you are the system administrator of, say, a school, you will be able to comfortably monitor the kids’ and teachers’ laptop batteries from your seat.

The battd daemon is available as a separate downloads and can be installed on an unlimited number of computers without restrictions.
